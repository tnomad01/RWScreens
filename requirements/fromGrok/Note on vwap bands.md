How Traders Use VWAP Deviations in Momentum Trading

1.  Trend Strength & Continuation:

•  In a strong uptrend (sloping VWAP upward), price holding above +1 SD or pulling back only to the +1 SD / VWAP area is bullish. Traders look for long entries on dips to these levels.

•  Price breaking and holding above +2 SD can signal powerful momentum (continuation trades).

2.  Mean Reversion (Pullbacks):

•  When price stretches far to +2 or +3 SD and shows signs of exhaustion (e.g., rejection candles), traders anticipate a reversion back toward VWAP or the opposite band.

•  In ranging markets (flat VWAP), the area between -1 SD and +1 SD is treated as value; bounces off the extremes are mean-reversion setups.

3.  Breakout Confirmation:

•  A decisive break above the upper band (especially with volume) can confirm a strong bullish breakout.

•  Conversely, breaking below the lower band signals bearish momentum.

4.  “Temperature Gauge” Analogy:

•  Many traders (including in order-flow contexts) view the bands as a thermometer: the farther price is from VWAP, the “hotter” (more extended) the move is.

Context matters a lot:

•  Trending day (sloped VWAP): Favor continuation trades toward the bands or pullbacks into value.

•  Choppy/ranging day (flat VWAP): Favor mean reversion at the outer bands.

•  Combine with price action, volume, EMAs, and overall market context — never use in isolation.

Relevance to Ross Cameron / Warrior Trading Momentum System

In Warrior Trading-style momentum setups, VWAP + standard deviation bands help:

•  Identify pullback entries in strong trends (e.g., buying dips to VWAP or +1 SD in an uptrend).

•  Gauge how extended a move has become (useful for scalping entries/exits or avoiding chasing overextended stocks).

•  Differentiate “Stage 1” trends (pullbacks often reach VWAP) from “Stage 2” explosive trends (pullbacks may only reach the 1 SD line).
